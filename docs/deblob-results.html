Why distance alone isn't enough

The central finding. Each strip is the structural distance to the nearest known schema, for one group of cases. If distance cleanly separated "new" from "same-but-drifted", the groups wouldn't overlap — but they do, right across the veto line.

● median▬ min–max range

The overlap is the point

drift/renamed reaches 0.29 while some new families sit as low as 0.0 — the two overlap straddling the 0.15 line. No single threshold separates them, so the deterministic veto handles the far-and-obvious (new families at 0.38+) and the model must make the semantic call in the middle. Deterministic lane necessary but insufficient; the model is non-redundant.

How it got here — five iterations

The first fine-tune (v1) mode-collapsed to always-match (0% new-schema). Two series: new-schema recall and overall decision accuracy, across the fix iterations.

new-schema recall decision accuracy

Fixes, in order: completion-only loss + balanced sampling (broke the collapse), finite hypothesis scoring (no hallucination), more data (decision 78%), deterministic distance veto (new-schema 100%). v4 and v3b are two operating points — safe-discovery vs. best-all-round.

All figures: the production-trained LoRA adapter (via deploy/experiment/modal/live_round.py) evaluated on 180 family-held-out cases from deblob-eval generate, finite-scoring + deterministic veto. Palette validated colorblind-safe. Honest caveat: single seed, synthetic construction-truth corpus, and the held-out set was consulted across iterations — a sealed multi-seed audit is the next step.
